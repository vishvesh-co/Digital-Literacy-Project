Case Study: Cyberbullying

Cyberbullying refers to the use of digital platforms — social media, messaging apps, online forums, and gaming environments — to harass, threaten, humiliate, or intimidate an individual repeatedly over time. Unlike physical bullying, cyberbullying can occur 24 hours a day, is often anonymous, and leaves a permanent digital record.

A realistic scenario: Riya, a first-year engineering student, joined a college WhatsApp group. After sharing her opinion on a classroom topic, several members began mocking her publicly in the group, sharing edited screenshots of her messages out of context, and circulating a fake profile impersonating her on Instagram. The harassment escalated to direct messages containing threats and insults from anonymous accounts. Within two weeks, Riya began skipping classes, withdrew from group activities, and reported symptoms of anxiety and depression to the college counsellor.

Cyberbullying typically targets individuals who appear different, express unconventional views, or who are new to a social environment and lack an established support network. Victims are often young people between the ages of 14 and 24. The consequences are severe: academic decline, social withdrawal, mental health deterioration, and in extreme cases, self-harm. For the perpetrators, there can be legal consequences under the Information Technology Act, 2000, and the Indian Penal Code, including charges related to criminal intimidation and defamation.
